Once upon a time people had ~ blogrolls ~ Well here in 2026, we have wander pages (a la [James' Coffee Blog](https://jamesg.blog/wander)).

- [A Collection of Unmitigated Pedantry](https://acoup.blog/) by Bret Devereaux: Do you like reading about the Roman Republic? Of course you do. Dr Devereaux is (imo) the leading public intellectual on the classic Mediterranean, not to mention fantasy worldbuilding; his _very_ long posts are scholarly but eminently readable.
- [Alan Jacobs](https://social.ayjay.org/): One of the greatest literary and cultural critics of our time, even if he _occasionally_ presumes the truth of his Christian faith.
- [anhvn](https://anhvn.com/): Anh is a designer (?) based in Vancouver (??) I think (???). But in any case she redesigns her site about once a month and it's always delightful.
- [Articles of Interest](https://articlesofinterest.substack.com/): Everybody's favorite podcast about clothes, why we wear them, and what they mean to us. Seriously Avery Trufelman is one of the best in the (podcast) biz.
- [Esoterica](https://www.youtube.com/@TheEsotericaChannel): Short educational videos from a PhD in Western esotericism. If you have any interest in the history of mysticism, occultism, or Western esotericism, you _must_ watch.
- [Futility Closet](https://www.futilitycloset.com/): A charming collection of fun facts and curiosities. Their podcast is dearly missed.
- [Gastropod](https://gastropod.com/): Delightfully nerdy podcast that looks at "food through the lens of science and history" — one of those podcasts where you learn something every episode, no matter how well you know the topic at hand.
- [Jacob Geller](https://www.youtube.com/@JacobGeller): The most important media critic of my generation, and it's not even close. You can skip the year's-best and "games that do x" video essays if you really don't care about video games, but everything else is essential.
- [Takes™ by Jamelle Bouie](https://www.youtube.com/@jamelle-bouie): The only American political commentator that makes a lick of sense.
- [Literature and History](https://literatureandhistory.com/): One of the greatest educational resources of the 21st century. An English PhD started with a desire to chronicle the evolution of English literature, starting in _ancient Mesopotamia_; 200+ hours later, he's finally made it to early Islam. Every episode is approachable but exhaustively researched; the episode on [ancient Zoroastrianism](https://literatureandhistory.com/episode-082-zoroastrianism/) is the best beginner's introduction to Zoroastrianism I'm aware of.
- [Naomi Kanakia](https://www.woman-of-letters.com/): A software engineer-turned-literary critic with a delightfully eclectic focus on the Great Books (and the Great Books-that-were-forgotten!).
- [Ones and Tooze](https://foreignpolicy.com/podcasts/ones-and-tooze/): Biweekly deep dives from [beloved economic historian](https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/adam-tooze-profile.html) Adam Tooze. Probably the best way to keep current on the world _outside_ the United States.
- [Quinns Quest](https://www.youtube.com/@Quinns_Quest): I've been following Quinns since the _Rock, Paper, Shotgun_ days, but he's truly found his calling nerding out about tabletop role-playing games while engaging in droll English humour. His enthusiasm is so infectious that I a.) watch every episode despite knowing I'll never play most if any of these games b.) want to go be enthusiastic about all the things I love too.
- [Robin Sloan](https://www.robinsloan.com/): C'mon if you've spent ten minutes on this site you know I read every word the man writes.
- [Tor's Cabinet of Curiosities](https://www.youtube.com/@torscabinetofcuriosities): A self-described "autistic special interests" channel where the host dives deep on bizarre historical oddities, like how [Mormonism almost had its own alphabet](https://youtube.com/watch?v=1d9wF38koOo) or how the Nation of Islam was (probably) founded by [a Central Asian tamale salesman from Oregon](https://youtube.com/watch?v=nIGceyJICwo), or low-stakes-but-hilarious Wikipedia drama like the decade-long fight over a photo of an economist sitting in a chair (the economist's name? [Guy Standing](https://youtube.com/watch?v=A48oR4Zc9ik)), or genuine mysteries like the well-documented existence of [blacks in the Caucasus](https://youtube.com/watch?v=uIoYvMhMG4w) or one of the more credible [mass UFO sightings](https://youtube.com/watch?v=VIwyW83-riA). Despite the low-budget, public-access-television vibes and the runtimes running close to an hour, I watch every episode almost immediately.
