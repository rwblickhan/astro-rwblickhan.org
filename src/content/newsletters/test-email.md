> Everyone has their own ladder. Some do their best to climb to the top, while others don't even realize they have a ladder. You came along... and helped me find my ladder.

From the opening moments, the audience knows that Jūzō Itami’s 1985 _Tampopo_ is not going to be like any other film they’ve ever seen.

The camera is set facing rows of half-filled theater seating, as if we are on screen and the audience is watching us. A white-clad gangster and his girlfriend sit down in the front row, and butlers bring in a feast for the lounging _yakuza_. Before he can dig in, however, he stands up and begins to lecture the audience. “You know how during movies some people eat potato chips and crinkle wrappers? \[...\] Make that noise after the film starts and I might kill you.”

From there, the audience is thrust into the tale of a wandering trucker who helps a middle-aged widower become the best ramen chef in Tokyo, frequently interrupted by comedic sketches exploring the human relationship to food and how that interrelates to the big universals of love, sex, and death.
